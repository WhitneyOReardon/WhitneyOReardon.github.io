Week 8 | Vinyl Cutting

This week I used the vinyl cutter to create a design on a sweatshirt. Vinyl cutting is a form of digital fabrication because it takes images, text, or other computer aided drawings and generates a cutting path which directly controls the movements and operations of a manufactuing tool. For the vinyl cutter, this equiptment is a subtractive manufacturing tool, since it cuts away at the material. Vinyl cutting fits the description of digital fabrication because it is a process that uses digital data, generated with the help of CAD and CAM softwares, to control a manufacturing machine.

Designing

Initially, when I was trying to come up with ideas of what to make I did some browsing on a couple of merchandise websites. I came across this mug that has a review of coffee mugs written on it.


I loved how weird and meta this design was, so that made me think of making a sweatshirt with the dictionary definition of sweatshirt on it. I looked at a few different online dictionaries to find the technical definition. Also, I used these sources to inform the physical layout of my design.






In Illustrator, I created the design. For my graphic, I made a little speaker icon, like the ones found in every online dictionary. I referenced a similar image of the icon.


I added the word "sweatshirt", with some phonetic pronunciations.


Here, I added the definition text in a dictionary-like layout. Also, I hadn't originally planned to include an example sentence, but I found this one "she was dressed casually in jeans and a sweatshirt" from the Cambridge Dictionary and it was just so perfect, I had to use it!


My first attempt to cut this text on the vinyl cutter did not go well. I realized that much of the text was too small, and some of the letters got distorted because of the movement of the blade and the sharp corners of the font.


From the previous attempt, I found that the smallest, legible font size was about 36 pt. Based off of this, I edited my text to be larger, which also meant using a shorter definition (this one is from Merriam-Webster Dictionary). In addition, I used a bold, rounded font for much of the smaller text so that the cut movements would not be so jagged. Lastly, I inverted the graphic to be more visible. I saved this design as a .jpeg file.

Cutting & Transfering

To program the vinyl cutter I used an application called "Sure Cuts A Lot 3 Pro". I opened my .jpeg file in this program on the laptop in the Fiber Lab. Because of the way the vinyl gets transfered to the fabric, I took the mirror image of my design before cutting it.


On the machine, I loaded a roll of white vinyl, making sure that it was unreeling from the top and that the loose edge was secured in three spots. Before cutting, I changed the force setting to 102 g, the recommended amount for apparel vinyl.


I peeled away the background part from my vinyl text. This left behind the interior parts of the graphic and letters. I weeded these out by hand, using an exacto blade.


At the heat press, I turned the temperature up to 350 F. After pressing my sweatshirt for 3 seconds alone, I placed my vinyl and pressed that for 10 seconds.




Here is my finished product! If I were to do this again, I would place the text lower and more to the center, but that's ok, I've learned something about tee shirt design. Overall, I'm very pleased with my sweatshirt!
